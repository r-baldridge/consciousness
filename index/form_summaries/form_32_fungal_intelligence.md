# Form 32: Fungal Networks & Mycorrhizal Intelligence

## Definition
Form 32 explores the cognitive and computational capabilities of fungal organisms, from mycorrhizal networks connecting forest ecosystems to slime molds demonstrating problem-solving, bridging mycology, network science, unconventional computing, and indigenous knowledge.

## Key Concepts
- Common Mycorrhizal Networks (CMNs) as the "Wood Wide Web" for inter-plant communication and resource transfer
- Slime mold (Physarum polycephalum) problem-solving: maze solving, network optimization, multi-objective balancing
- Chemical communication via 300+ volatile organic compounds for defense, recognition, and inter-kingdom signaling
- Electrical signaling analogous to neural action potentials with possible 50-word "vocabulary"
- Fungal computing: mycelium as logical gates, memristors, sensors, and memory devices
- Entheogenic species and their role in indigenous consciousness practices
- Host behavioral manipulation (Ophiocordyceps zombie ant fungi)

## Core Methods & Mechanisms
- Network topology analysis of mycorrhizal systems for resource-sharing and communication patterns
- Slime mold optimization experiments (shortest path, Tokyo rail recreation, efficiency vs. robustness)
- Electrical signal recording and decoding across fungal hyphae
- Bio-inspired algorithm development for transportation planning, robot swarm coordination, and distributed decision-making
- Integration of indigenous ceremonial traditions (Mazatec velada, Siberian Amanita, Aboriginal Australian, Traditional Chinese Medicine)

## Unique Contributions
Form 32 represents a paradigm shift in understanding intelligence as a distributed, networked phenomenon emerging without centralized nervous systems. It uniquely demonstrates that computational problem-solving, memory, and communication can arise from hyphal networks and single-celled organisms, while also preserving indigenous traditions spanning thousands of years of human-fungal relationship.

### Research Highlights
- Nakagaki (2000): Physarum slime mold solves mazes, finding shortest paths -- landmark demonstration of non-neural computation
- Adamatzky: Fungal mycelium as unconventional computing substrates -- logical gates, memristors, and electrical spike patterns with up to 50 distinct classes
- Simard: Wood Wide Web discovery showing CMNs redistribute carbon preferentially to shaded seedlings and kin
- Bebber et al. (2007): Biological transport network design principles derived from fungal growth patterns
- Electrical spike recordings reveal 0.03-2.1 mV amplitude signals lasting 1-21 hours, with vocabulary-like pattern diversity
