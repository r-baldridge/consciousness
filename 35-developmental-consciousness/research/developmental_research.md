# Form 35: Developmental Consciousness - Comprehensive Research Documentation

## Overview

Form 35 addresses the emergence, transformation, and decline of consciousness across the human lifespan. This encompasses the ontogenesis of awareness from prenatal development through the final moments of life, integrating developmental psychology, cognitive neuroscience, neurophenomenology, and end-of-life studies.

Understanding developmental consciousness is crucial for several reasons:
1. **Consciousness Origins**: Investigating when and how consciousness first emerges
2. **Capacity Tracking**: Mapping cognitive capacities as they develop and change
3. **Ethical Implications**: Informing decisions about fetal rights, pediatric care, and end-of-life
4. **Integration with Other Forms**: Establishing baseline capacities for Forms 10-27
5. **Intervention Timing**: Understanding critical periods for therapeutic interventions

## Part I: Scientific Foundation

### 1.1 Key Researchers and Their Contributions

#### Jean Piaget (1896-1980)
**Foundational Contribution**: Theory of cognitive development stages

**Key Works**:
- *The Origins of Intelligence in Children* (1936)
- *The Construction of Reality in the Child* (1937)
- *The Child's Conception of the World* (1929)

**Core Concepts**:
- **Sensorimotor Stage** (0-2 years): Intelligence through action and sensory experience
- **Preoperational Stage** (2-7 years): Symbolic thinking, egocentrism
- **Concrete Operational Stage** (7-11 years): Logical thinking about concrete events
- **Formal Operational Stage** (11+ years): Abstract and hypothetical reasoning

**Relevance to Consciousness**: Piaget mapped the emergence of representational thought, which is fundamental to self-aware consciousness. His concept of "decentration" (moving from egocentric to perspectival thinking) directly relates to theory of mind development.

#### Alison Gopnik (1955-)
**Foundational Contribution**: Child as scientist paradigm; infant cognition research

**Key Works**:
- *The Philosophical Baby* (2009)
- *The Scientist in the Crib* (1999, with Meltzoff and Kuhl)
- *The Gardener and the Carpenter* (2016)

**Core Concepts**:
- **Bayesian Learning**: Infants use probabilistic reasoning
- **Lantern vs. Spotlight Consciousness**: Infants have diffuse, open attention (lantern) vs. adult focused attention (spotlight)
- **Imagination and Counterfactual Reasoning**: Early childhood capacity for alternative possibilities
- **Causal Learning**: Infants as active experimenters

**Key Findings**:
- 15-month-olds understand others' preferences can differ from their own
- Children engage in sophisticated hypothesis testing
- Play serves crucial epistemic functions
- Theory of mind emerges gradually through social interaction

#### Elizabeth Spelke (1949-)
**Foundational Contribution**: Core knowledge systems in infants

**Key Works**:
- "Core Knowledge" (2000, American Psychologist)
- "Origins of Knowledge" (1992, Psychological Review)
- Multiple studies on infant cognition at Harvard Lab for Developmental Studies

**Core Concepts**:
- **Core Knowledge Systems**: Innate systems for objects, numbers, agents, geometry
- **Object Representation**: Infants expect objects to be solid, persist through occlusion
- **Number Sense**: Approximate number system present from birth
- **Geometric Navigation**: Innate spatial reasoning capacities
- **Intentional Agency Detection**: Recognition of goal-directed behavior

**Key Findings**:
- 4-month-olds have object permanence expectations (earlier than Piaget claimed)
- Infants distinguish animate from inanimate entities
- Core systems are shared across cultures and with other species
- Core knowledge provides foundation for later conceptual development

#### Renee Baillargeon (1954-)
**Foundational Contribution**: Violation-of-expectation paradigm; infant physical reasoning

**Key Works**:
- "Object Permanence in 3.5- and 4.5-Month-Old Infants" (1987)
- "Reasoning about Containment Events in Very Young Infants" (1994)
- "Physical Reasoning in Infancy" (2004, with colleagues)

**Core Concepts**:
- **Violation-of-Expectation (VOE)**: Infants look longer at impossible events
- **Physical Reasoning**: Infants understand support, containment, covering, collision
- **Event Categories**: Infants form categories of physical events with specific variables
- **Learning-in-Context**: Perceptual learning guides physical reasoning development

**Key Findings**:
- 2.5-month-olds surprised when objects pass through each other
- Physical reasoning develops hierarchically within event categories
- Infants identify variables relevant to predictions about physical events
- Earlier competence than behavioral measures suggested

#### Michael Tomasello (1950-)
**Foundational Contribution**: Shared intentionality and cultural learning

**Key Works**:
- *The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition* (1999)
- *Origins of Human Communication* (2008)
- *A Natural History of Human Thinking* (2014)

**Core Concepts**:
- **Shared Intentionality**: Understanding others as intentional agents with shareable goals
- **Joint Attention**: Coordinated attention between self, other, and object
- **Cultural Learning**: Imitative, instructed, and collaborative learning
- **Recursive Mind-Reading**: Understanding that others understand that you understand

**Key Findings**:
- 9-12 month revolution in social cognition (pointing, gaze following)
- Human-specific capacity for shared intentionality enables cumulative culture
- Collaboration and communication co-evolved
- Moral psychology emerges from shared intentionality

#### Bruce Greyson (1946-)
**Foundational Contribution**: Near-death experience research and scale development

**Key Works**:
- *After: A Doctor Explores What Near-Death Experiences Reveal about Life and Beyond* (2021)
- "The Near-Death Experience Scale" (1983)
- Multiple studies in Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease, Lancet

**Core Concepts**:
- **Greyson NDE Scale**: 16-item standardized measure of NDE features
- **Core NDE Features**: Peace, light, life review, deceased relatives, border
- **Transformative Effects**: Long-term positive psychological changes
- **Consciousness-Brain Independence**: Evidence for consciousness during minimal brain activity

**Key Findings**:
- NDEs occur across cultures with consistent core features
- Cardiac arrest NDEs challenge materialist consciousness theories
- Long-term positive changes in values and beliefs
- Different from hallucinations in phenomenology and aftereffects

#### Additional Key Researchers

**Andrew Meltzoff**: Neonatal imitation, "like me" framework
**Patricia Kuhl**: Language acquisition, social gating of learning
**Karen Wynn**: Infant mathematical cognition, moral evaluation
**Paul Bloom**: Infant moral psychology, essence concepts
**Susan Carey**: Conceptual change, number cognition
**Uta Frith**: Autism and theory of mind development
**Simon Baron-Cohen**: Mind-reading development, autism
**Henry Wellman**: Theory of mind development sequence
**Philippe Rochat**: Self-awareness development, ecological self
**Vasudevi Reddy**: Second-person approach to infant consciousness

### 1.2 Fetal Consciousness Research

#### Neurodevelopmental Timeline

**Weeks 3-4 (Embryonic)**:
- Neural tube formation
- No neural activity

**Weeks 5-8**:
- Brain vesicle differentiation
- First neurons appear (~week 5)
- Spontaneous neural activity begins (~week 8)

**Weeks 8-12 (First Trimester)**:
- Thalamocortical connections begin forming
- Reflexive movements (not evidence of consciousness)
- Basic neural architecture established

**Weeks 12-20 (Second Trimester Early)**:
- Subplate zone develops (temporary cortical structure)
- Pain pathways still incomplete
- Sleep-like states predominate
- Consciousness unlikely due to:
  - Incomplete thalamocortical connectivity
  - Chemical sedation from placenta
  - Lack of organized cortical activity

**Weeks 20-28 (Second Trimester Late)**:
- **Critical Period**: Thalamocortical connections mature (~24 weeks)
- EEG patterns emerge showing brain activity coordination
- Pain perception becomes possible (~24-26 weeks)
- Minimal consciousness potential begins
- Fetus can:
  - Respond to sounds
  - Show habituation (basic learning)
  - Display sleep-wake cycles

**Weeks 28-40 (Third Trimester)**:
- Increasing cortical maturation
- REM sleep emerges
- Learning demonstrated:
  - Familiarization with mother's voice
  - Recognition of stories read during pregnancy (DeCasper & Spence, 1986)
  - Musical memory
- Sensory awareness likely present (muted by uterine environment)
- **"Womb consciousness"**: Minimal awareness in sedated state

#### Key Research Findings

**DeCasper & Fifer (1980)**: Newborns prefer mother's voice
- Implies prenatal auditory learning
- Sucking rate paradigm demonstration

**Hepper (1991)**: Fetal music recognition
- Familiarized fetuses with TV theme
- Recognition persisted postnatally

**Lecanuet et al. (1993)**: Fetal response to speech
- Heart rate changes to familiar vs. novel sounds
- Suggests discriminatory processing

**Lagercrantz & Changeux (2009)**: "The Emergence of Human Consciousness"
- Argued consciousness impossible before ~24 weeks
- Thalamocortical maturation essential
- Neurochemical sedation hypothesis

**Derbyshire & Bockmann (2020)**: Fetal pain evidence
- Review concluded pain possible 13-18 weeks
- Challenged 24-week threshold
- Ethical implications for fetal surgery, abortion

#### Consciousness Assessment Challenges

**Methodological Limitations**:
- Cannot use verbal report
- Behavioral measures confounded by reflexes
- Imaging difficult in utero
- Sedation state hard to separate from consciousness absence

**Theoretical Challenges**:
- What counts as consciousness without self-reflection?
- Is sensory awareness sufficient?
- Role of memory in consciousness
- Difference between potential and actual consciousness

### 1.3 Infant Cognition Studies

#### Looking-Time Paradigms

**Habituation-Dishabituation**:
- Present stimulus repeatedly until looking decreases
- Present novel stimulus
- Increased looking = discrimination
- Used to assess: Category formation, memory, surprise

**Violation of Expectation (VOE)**:
- Present expected vs. unexpected events
- Longer looking at unexpected = implicit knowledge
- Example: Object passes through solid wall
- Baillargeon's pioneering work

**Preferential Looking**:
- Present two stimuli simultaneously
- Measure looking time to each
- Preference indicates discrimination
- Used for face processing, social preferences

**Visual Cliff**:
- Depth perception assessment
- Crawling infants avoid apparent drop
- Emotional referencing from caregiver

#### Key Cognitive Domains Studied

**Object Cognition**:
- Permanence (earlier than Piaget: 2.5-4 months by VOE)
- Solidity, continuity, contact causality
- Numerical reasoning with small sets

**Social Cognition**:
- Face preference (newborn)
- Gaze following (6-12 months)
- Joint attention (9-12 months)
- Intentionality understanding (9+ months)
- Theory of mind precursors

**Language Cognition**:
- Phoneme discrimination (newborn, narrows by 12 months)
- Word segmentation (7-8 months)
- Word-object mapping (8-12 months)
- Statistical learning

**Causal Cognition**:
- Contact causality (6 months)
- Agent causation (early first year)
- Interventional understanding (toddler period)

#### Neuroimaging in Infants

**Electroencephalography (EEG)**:
- Non-invasive, portable
- Excellent temporal resolution
- Event-related potentials (ERPs) for cognitive processing
- Used for language, face, and object processing studies

**Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS)**:
- Measures blood oxygenation
- Tolerant of movement
- Good spatial resolution
- Face processing, language lateralization

**Functional MRI (fMRI)**:
- Challenging in awake infants
- Sleep studies possible
- Reveals network architecture
- Shows language network lateralization in infants

**Key Findings**:
- Adult-like network architecture present in infancy
- Functional connectivity predicts later cognitive outcomes
- Event-related responses to novelty, faces, language
- Prefrontal engagement in cognitive tasks

### 1.4 Theory of Mind Development

#### Developmental Sequence (Wellman & Liu, 2004)

**Stage 1: Diverse Desires** (~18 months)
- Understanding others can want different things
- "He likes carrots, I don't like carrots"

**Stage 2: Diverse Beliefs** (~2-3 years)
- Understanding others can have different beliefs (when both true)
- "She thinks it's in the drawer, I think it's on the table"

**Stage 3: Knowledge Access** (~3-4 years)
- Understanding that seeing leads to knowing
- "She saw inside the box, so she knows"

**Stage 4: False Belief** (~4-5 years)
- Understanding others can hold false beliefs
- Classic Sally-Anne task
- **Key milestone for full theory of mind**

**Stage 5: Hidden Emotion** (~5-6 years)
- Understanding people can feel differently than they appear
- Emotional regulation and display rules

#### Classic Paradigms

**Sally-Anne Task (Baron-Cohen et al., 1985)**:
- Sally puts marble in basket
- Sally leaves; Anne moves marble to box
- "Where will Sally look for her marble?"
- 4-year-olds succeed; 3-year-olds fail
- Autistic children show delays

**Smarties Task (Perner et al., 1987)**:
- Smarties tube actually contains pencils
- "What did you think was in here?"
- Own past false belief test

**Appearance-Reality Distinction (Flavell)**:
- Rock that looks like sponge
- "What does it look like? What is it really?"
- 3-year-olds confuse appearance and reality

#### Implicit vs. Explicit Theory of Mind

**Implicit Theory of Mind** (present earlier):
- Anticipatory looking (15 months) - Onishi & Baillargeon, 2005
- Looking time at false belief scenarios (13 months)
- Suggests earlier understanding than explicit tasks

**Explicit Theory of Mind**:
- Requires verbal report/action choice
- Executive function demands
- Language requirements
- Full competence 4-5 years

**Debate**: Is implicit theory of mind "real" theory of mind or simpler behavior-reading?

#### Neural Basis

**Key Brain Regions**:
- Temporoparietal Junction (TPJ): Belief representation
- Medial Prefrontal Cortex (mPFC): Mentalizing, self-other distinction
- Posterior Superior Temporal Sulcus (pSTS): Biological motion, intentionality
- Precuneus: Self-reflection, perspective taking

**Development**:
- TPJ shows increasing activation with age during theory of mind tasks
- mPFC-TPJ connectivity increases developmentally
- Right hemisphere initially dominant, becomes bilateral

### 1.5 Consciousness in Aging and Dementia

#### Normal Cognitive Aging

**Preserved Functions**:
- Vocabulary and semantic knowledge
- Procedural memory
- Emotional regulation
- Wisdom and life experience integration

**Declining Functions**:
- Processing speed
- Working memory capacity
- Episodic memory encoding
- Inhibitory control
- Divided attention

**Consciousness Implications**:
- Slower integration of information
- Reduced metacognitive monitoring
- Preserved experiential richness
- Temporal consciousness may shift (time seems faster)

#### Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI)

**Characteristics**:
- Memory complaints beyond normal aging
- Objective cognitive impairment (1-1.5 SD below norm)
- Preserved daily functioning
- Conversion to dementia: ~10-15% per year

**Consciousness Features**:
- Intact awareness of deficits initially
- Self-consciousness about lapses
- Anxiety about cognitive status
- Metacognitive monitoring preserved

#### Alzheimer's Disease Stages

**Stage 1: Preclinical** (biomarker positive, no symptoms)
- Consciousness appears normal
- Subtle objective deficits on testing
- No subjective awareness

**Stage 2: Early/Mild**
- Episodic memory loss predominates
- Awareness of deficits (insight preserved)
- Anxiety, depression common
- Self-consciousness affected but present
- Autobiographical memory begins eroding

**Stage 3: Moderate**
- **Anosognosia**: Loss of awareness of deficits
- Temporal disorientation
- Confabulation emerges
- Narrative identity fragmenting
- Living increasingly in "eternal present"
- Procedural and emotional memory preserved

**Stage 4: Severe**
- Profound memory loss
- Loss of autobiographical self
- Non-verbal communication predominates
- Emotional responsiveness often preserved
- "Being" without "self"
- Pleasure/pain still experienced
- Music, touch, familiar faces still evoke response

**Consciousness Considerations**:
- When is "self" lost vs. transformed?
- Emotional/embodied consciousness persists longer than narrative
- Personhood debates: Is the person still "there"?
- Quality of experience in late dementia

#### Terminal Lucidity

**Definition**: Unexpected return of mental clarity and memory shortly before death in patients with severe neurological disorders

**Reported Features**:
- Occurs hours to days before death
- Patient recognizes family members previously forgotten
- Coherent speech after prolonged silence
- Recovery of personality characteristics
- Followed by rapid decline and death

**Scientific Documentation**:
- Nahm et al. (2012): Systematic review
- Case reports across cultures and historical periods
- Most common in dementia, brain tumors, strokes

**Theoretical Significance**:
- Challenges strictly localized view of memory/consciousness
- Suggests brain may inhibit rather than produce some consciousness
- Possible surge of neuromodulators near death
- Alternative: Relaxation of pathological inhibition

## Part II: Developmental Stages of Consciousness

### 2.1 Prenatal Stages (18 stages total)

#### Stage 1: Pre-Neural (Weeks 0-3)
- **Biological Status**: Fertilization through early cell division
- **Neural Development**: No nervous system
- **Consciousness Status**: No consciousness possible
- **Sentience**: None

#### Stage 2: Neural Tube Formation (Weeks 3-4)
- **Biological Status**: Neural plate folds into tube
- **Neural Development**: Foundation for CNS established
- **Consciousness Status**: No consciousness
- **Sentience**: None

#### Stage 3: Brain Vesicle Stage (Weeks 4-6)
- **Biological Status**: Primary brain vesicles form
- **Neural Development**: Forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain differentiate
- **Consciousness Status**: No consciousness
- **Sentience**: None

#### Stage 4: Neurogenesis Begins (Weeks 6-10)
- **Biological Status**: Rapid neuron production
- **Neural Development**: Neurons migrating, some basic reflexes
- **Consciousness Status**: No consciousness - reflexes only
- **Sentience**: None

#### Stage 5: Early Fetal Period (Weeks 10-14)
- **Biological Status**: Fetal form established
- **Neural Development**: Brain growing rapidly, subplate forming
- **Consciousness Status**: No consciousness
- **Sensory Status**: Touch reflexes, no organized processing
- **Sentience**: None

#### Stage 6: Mid-Fetal Period (Weeks 14-18)
- **Biological Status**: Movement, thumb-sucking
- **Neural Development**: Thalamic connections beginning
- **Consciousness Status**: No consciousness (sedated state)
- **Sensory Status**: Vestibular, tactile processing emerging
- **Sentience**: Possibly minimal (controversial)

#### Stage 7: Thalamocortical Development (Weeks 18-22)
- **Biological Status**: Viability threshold approaching
- **Neural Development**: Thalamocortical connections forming
- **Consciousness Status**: Pre-conscious (substrate developing)
- **Sensory Status**: Auditory brainstem responses
- **Sentience**: Minimal sentience possible toward end

#### Stage 8: Viability Threshold (Weeks 22-24)
- **Biological Status**: Extra-uterine survival possible
- **Neural Development**: Thalamocortical connectivity sufficient for minimal cortical processing
- **Consciousness Status**: **EMERGENCE THRESHOLD**
- **Sensory Status**: Pain pathways functional
- **Sentience**: Minimal awareness likely present
- **Features**: Sleep states, startle responses, habituation

#### Stage 9: Third Trimester Early (Weeks 24-28)
- **Biological Status**: Rapid brain growth
- **Neural Development**: Cortical folding, synaptogenesis
- **Consciousness Status**: Minimal consciousness in sedated state
- **Sensory Status**: Hears sounds, responds to stimuli
- **Learning**: Habituation, voice familiarization
- **Sleep**: Sleep-wake cycles establish

#### Stage 10: Third Trimester Middle (Weeks 28-32)
- **Biological Status**: Fat accumulation, lung maturation
- **Neural Development**: Continued cortical elaboration
- **Consciousness Status**: Awareness expanding but muted
- **Sensory Status**: Visual response to light through abdomen
- **Learning**: Recognizes stories, music
- **REM Sleep**: Emerges with possible primitive dreaming

#### Stage 11: Third Trimester Late (Weeks 32-36)
- **Biological Status**: Near full-term
- **Neural Development**: Myelination accelerating
- **Consciousness Status**: Fetal consciousness established but sedated
- **Sensory Status**: Full sensory capacity (muted by environment)
- **Memory**: Prenatal memories forming
- **State**: Predominantly sleep-like

#### Stage 12: Pre-Birth/Term (Weeks 36-40)
- **Biological Status**: Full term
- **Neural Development**: Ready for extrauterine life
- **Consciousness Status**: Ready for consciousness transition
- **Preparation**: Birth rehearsal behaviors
- **Memory**: Carries prenatal learning into postnatal life

### 2.2 Neonatal Period (Birth - 28 days)

#### Stage 13: Birth Transition (Day 0-3)
- **Event**: The "great awakening" - consciousness flooding in
- **Consciousness Shift**: From muted fetal awareness to overwhelming sensory input
- **Neural Status**: Noradrenergic surge at birth
- **Key Features**:
  - First breath, temperature change, light exposure
  - Stress response massive
  - Already recognizes mother's voice, smell
  - Visual focus ~8-12 inches (face distance)
- **William James**: "Blooming, buzzing confusion" (likely overstated)

#### Stage 14: Early Neonatal (Days 3-14)
- **Consciousness Type**: Primary sensory awareness
- **Sleep**: 16-17 hours/day, brief wake periods
- **Attention**: Face preference, high contrast patterns
- **Social**: Imitation of facial expressions (Meltzoff)
- **Emotional**: Distress/contentment discrimination
- **Memory**: Recognition memory for mother's face (within hours)

#### Stage 15: Late Neonatal (Days 14-28)
- **Consciousness Type**: Emerging social awareness
- **Visual**: Tracking improves, prefers face-like patterns
- **Auditory**: Discriminates native language phonemes
- **Social**: Beginning social smile
- **Learning**: Conditioning, expectation formation
- **State Regulation**: Sleep patterns consolidating

### 2.3 Infant Stages (1-24 months)

#### Stage 16: Early Infant I (1-3 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: Sensory-affective awareness
- **Vision**: Color vision developing, depth perception emerging
- **Social**: Social smiling (6-8 weeks), enjoys faces
- **Motor**: Head control improving
- **Emotional**: Differentiated emotions (joy, anger, sadness)
- **Cognition**: Expecting contingent responses
- **Memory**: 1-2 day retention
- **Self**: No self-recognition

#### Stage 17: Early Infant II (3-6 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: Object-directed awareness emerging
- **Vision**: Full color, depth perception
- **Object Knowledge**: Solidity expectations, basic persistence
- **Social**: Stranger wariness beginning, clear attachment preferences
- **Motor**: Reaching, grasping intentionally
- **Cognition**: Causal expectation (contact causality)
- **Memory**: Multi-week retention
- **Vocalization**: Babbling begins
- **Self**: Ecological self (body as agent in world)

#### Stage 18: Middle Infant I (6-9 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: Intentional awareness
- **Object Permanence**: Searches for hidden objects (A not B error persists)
- **Social**: Joint attention emerging, gaze following
- **Motor**: Sitting, early crawling
- **Communication**: Communicative intent, pointing begins
- **Emotion**: Social referencing
- **Memory**: Deferred imitation (24+ hours)
- **Self**: Agent causation awareness

#### Stage 19: Middle Infant II (9-12 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: **JOINT INTENTIONALITY EMERGENCE**
- **Milestone**: "9-month revolution" in social cognition
- **Object Permanence**: Full Stage 4 (A not B solved)
- **Social**: Shared intentionality, triadic interaction
- **Communication**: First words, rich gesture use
- **Cognition**: Means-end problem solving
- **Emotion**: Complex emotions (shame, pride emerging)
- **Memory**: Deferred imitation over weeks
- **Self**: Others have intentions, early perspective differences

#### Stage 20: Late Infant I (12-18 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: Symbolic awareness emerging
- **Language**: Vocabulary explosion beginning, 50+ words
- **Object Permanence**: Invisible displacement mastered
- **Cognition**: Mental representation, symbolic play begins
- **Social**: Cooperation, helping behavior
- **Emotion**: Self-conscious emotions developing
- **Memory**: Autobiographical memory traces begin
- **Self**: Body self-recognition (but not mirror test yet)

#### Stage 21: Late Infant II (18-24 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: **SELF-RECOGNITION THRESHOLD**
- **Mirror Test**: Passes at 18-24 months
- **Language**: Two-word combinations, 200+ words
- **Cognition**: Mental problem-solving, deferred imitation
- **Theory of Mind**: Diverse desires understood
- **Emotion**: Pride, shame, embarrassment
- **Memory**: Explicit episodic memories forming
- **Self**: "I," "me," "mine" - explicit self-concept
- **Landmark**: Clear evidence of self-aware consciousness

### 2.4 Toddler Stage (2-3 years)

#### Stage 22: Early Toddler (24-30 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: Egocentric symbolic awareness
- **Language**: Sentence use, 500+ words
- **Cognition**: Dual representation (symbols stand for things)
- **Theory of Mind**: Others have beliefs (when both true)
- **Play**: Symbolic/pretend play flourishes
- **Emotion**: Emotional regulation challenges
- **Memory**: Autobiographical memories, but childhood amnesia barrier emerging
- **Self**: Possessive self, "mine"

#### Stage 23: Late Toddler (30-36 months)
- **Consciousness Type**: Emerging narrative consciousness
- **Language**: Full sentences, past/future reference
- **Cognition**: Some logical reasoning (in concrete contexts)
- **Theory of Mind**: Knowledge-ignorance distinction
- **Play**: Complex pretend scenarios
- **Emotion**: Labels emotions, beginning regulation
- **Memory**: Participating in memory narratives with adults
- **Self**: Temporal self-extension (knows own past/future)

---

### 2.5 Early Childhood (3-6 years)

#### Stage 24: Early Preschool (3-4 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Pre-operational awareness
- **Language**: Full grammar, complex sentences
- **Cognition**: Classification, counting, causality
- **Theory of Mind**: **FALSE BELIEF THRESHOLD** (4 years)
- **Memory**: Childhood amnesia offset (~3.5 years)
- **Self**: Narrative self emerging, describes self with traits
- **Metacognition**: Beginning awareness of own thinking
- **Dreams**: Reports dreams, may confuse dream/reality

#### Stage 25: Late Preschool (4-5 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Full naive theory of mind
- **Theory of Mind**: False belief mastered
- **Language**: 2000+ words, metalinguistic awareness
- **Cognition**: Conservation failures, but improving
- **Memory**: Reliable autobiographical memory
- **Self**: Psychological self (thoughts, feelings as defining)
- **Metacognition**: Knows knowing vs. guessing
- **Dreams**: Knows dreams aren't real

#### Stage 26: Kindergarten Age (5-6 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Transitional to operational
- **Theory of Mind**: Hidden emotion understood
- **Cognition**: Improved conservation, seriation
- **Memory**: Source monitoring improving
- **Self**: Social comparison self-evaluation
- **Metacognition**: Knows limits of own knowledge
- **Moral**: Beginnings of fairness reasoning

---

### 2.6 Middle Childhood (6-12 years)

#### Stage 27: Early Elementary (6-8 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Concrete operational awareness
- **Cognition**: Conservation mastered, logical operations
- **Theory of Mind**: Recursive theory of mind emerging
- **Memory**: Elaborated autobiographical narratives
- **Self**: Competence-based self-concept
- **Metacognition**: Explicit memory strategies
- **Moral**: Rule-based moral reasoning

#### Stage 28: Middle Elementary (8-10 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Expanding concrete operations
- **Cognition**: Classification, multiple perspectives
- **Theory of Mind**: Second-order false belief ("She thinks that he thinks...")
- **Memory**: Strategic memory use
- **Self**: Trait-based self-concept, stable across contexts
- **Metacognition**: Monitoring and control of learning
- **Time Consciousness**: Longer time horizons, future orientation

#### Stage 29: Late Elementary/Pre-Adolescent (10-12 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Transitional to abstract
- **Cognition**: Early abstract reasoning in familiar domains
- **Theory of Mind**: Understanding complex social dynamics
- **Memory**: Adult-like episodic memory
- **Self**: Integration of multiple self-concepts
- **Metacognition**: Sophisticated strategy selection
- **Identity**: Pre-identity exploration

### 2.7 Adolescent Consciousness (12-18 years)

#### Stage 30: Early Adolescence (12-14 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: **FORMAL OPERATIONAL EMERGENCE**
- **Cognition**: Hypothetical-deductive reasoning
- **Brain**: Prefrontal pruning, myelination surge
- **Self**: Imaginary audience, personal fable
- **Identity**: Identity vs. role confusion begins
- **Metacognition**: Recursive self-reflection
- **Risk**: Reward sensitivity peaks, executive control lagging
- **Time**: Extended future time perspective

#### Stage 31: Middle Adolescence (14-16 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Abstract self-consciousness
- **Cognition**: Abstract reasoning in multiple domains
- **Brain**: Continued prefrontal development
- **Self**: Introspection intensifies
- **Identity**: Active exploration
- **Emotion**: Intense, labile
- **Metacognition**: Can reflect on reflection
- **Social**: Peer consciousness paramount

#### Stage 32: Late Adolescence (16-18 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Emerging adult awareness
- **Cognition**: Full formal operations
- **Brain**: Prefrontal nearing adult levels
- **Self**: Integration of multiple identities
- **Identity**: Commitment formation
- **Future Orientation**: Career/life planning
- **Metacognition**: Adult-like self-regulation
- **Moral**: Post-conventional reasoning possible

### 2.8 Adult Stages (18-65 years)

#### Stage 33: Emerging Adulthood (18-25 years)
- **Consciousness Type**: Post-formal exploration
- **Brain**: Prefrontal reaches full maturity (~25)
- **Cognition**: Dialectical thinking emerging
- **Identity**: Continued exploration, commitment formation
- **Self**: Individuation from family of origin

## Part IV: Research Paradigms

### 4.1 Looking-Time Studies

**Principle**: Infants look longer at novel, unexpected, or preferred stimuli

**Variants**:

**Habituation-Dishabituation**:
```
1. Present stimulus A repeatedly
2. Measure looking time decrease (habituation)
3. Present stimulus B
4. Increased looking = discrimination of A and B
```

**Violation of Expectation**:
```
1. Familiarize with possible event
2. Present possible vs. impossible test event
3. Longer looking at impossible = expected possibility
```

**Preferential Looking**:
```
1. Present two stimuli simultaneously
2. Measure looking time to each
3. Preference indicates discrimination and/or preference
```

**Head-Turn Preference**:
```
1. Auditory stimuli from different sides
2. Turning toward stimulus = preference
3. Used for language, music studies
```

### 4.2 Violation of Expectation Details

**Original Drawbridge Study** (Baillargeon et al., 1985):
- Infants habituated to drawbridge rotating 180 degrees
- Test: Drawbridge with block behind it
  - Possible: Stops at block
  - Impossible: Passes through block position
- Result: 4.5-month-olds looked longer at impossible event
- Interpretation: Infants have object permanence earlier than Piaget claimed

**Criticisms and Responses**:
- Low-level perceptual preferences? (Controlled in later studies)
- What does looking "mean"? (Replication with different measures)
- Implicit vs. explicit knowledge? (Legitimate distinction)

### 4.3 Preferential Looking Details

**Face Preference Studies**:
- Newborns prefer face-like patterns
- 2-month-olds: preference for mother's face
- 3-month-olds: own-race preference (can be modified)

**Social Preference Studies** (Hamlin et al.):
- 6-month-olds prefer helpful over hindering characters
- Basis for "baby morality" claims

**Linguistic Studies**:
- Native language preference by 4 months
- Phoneme discrimination narrowing by 12 months

### 4.4 Neuroimaging in Developmental Research

#### EEG/ERP Methods

**Advantages for Infants**:
- Non-invasive
- Tolerates movement (to a degree)
- High temporal resolution
- Portable

**Key ERP Components in Infants**:
- **Nc (Negative central)**: Attention allocation
- **Pb (Positive slow wave)**: Recognition/memory
- **N290/P400**: Face processing (precursors to adult N170)

**Paradigm Example**:
```
Face Processing Study:
1. Present faces and objects
2. Record ERP
3. Compare N290 amplitude: larger for faces
4. Conclusion: Specialized face processing exists in infancy
```

#### fNIRS Methods

**Advantages**:
- Spatial localization (better than EEG)
- Movement tolerance
- Can use with awake infants
- Non-invasive

**Applications**:
- Language lateralization in infants
- Face processing regions
- Social brain development

#### Infant fMRI

**Challenges**:
- Movement artifacts
- Noise tolerance
- Need for sleep or sedation
- Ethical concerns

**Successes**:
- Sleep studies showing adult-like network architecture
- Resting-state connectivity predicts outcomes
- Language network visible in infants

### 4.5 Behavioral Markers of Consciousness

**For Infants**:
- Looking time as proxy for surprise/expectation
- Preferential reaching
- Anticipatory behavior
- Physiological responses (heart rate, pupil dilation)

**For Toddlers/Children**:
- Verbal report (with limitations)
- Game/task performance
- Spontaneous language
- Drawing, play

**For Theory of Mind**:
- Sally-Anne task and variants
- Appearance-reality distinction
- Deception paradigms

## Part V: End-of-Life Consciousness

### 5.1 Near-Death Experiences

#### Definition and Features

**Definition**: Profound psychological experiences occurring during actual or threatened death, involving altered consciousness

**Core Features (Greyson Scale)**:
1. **Cognitive Features**:
   - Time distortion
   - Thought acceleration
   - Life review (panoramic memory)
   - Sudden understanding

2. **Affective Features**:
   - Peace and well-being
   - Joy
   - Cosmic unity
   - Seeing/feeling divine light

3. **Paranormal Features**:
   - Vivid sensory experiences
   - Apparent ESP
   - Precognitive visions
   - "Out of body" experiences

4. **Transcendental Features**:
   - Unearthly realm
   - Mystical beings/deceased relatives
   - Border or limit
   - Decision or being told to return

#### Research Evidence

**Prevalence**:
- 10-20% of cardiac arrest survivors report NDEs
- Similar across cultures (with cultural coloring)
- Occur in children and adults

**AWARE Study** (Parnia et al., 2014):
- Prospective study of cardiac arrest survivors
- 9% reported NDE features
- 2% had visual awareness during CPR (when flat EEG)
- Supports consciousness during minimal brain activity

**van Lommel et al. (2001)**: Lancet study
- 344 cardiac arrest patients
- 18% reported NDE
- No medical predictors (not hypoxia, medications)
- Long-term positive psychological changes

#### Theoretical Interpretations

**Physiological Theories**:
- Hypoxia, hypercarbia (oxygen/CO2 levels)
- REM intrusion
- Endorphin release
- Temporal lobe seizures
- DMT release

**Problems with Physiological Theories**:
- NDEs occur without hypoxia
- Clarity unlike hypoxic confusion
- Drug-induced hallucinations different phenomenology
- Reports during flat EEG

**Psychological Theories**:
- Expectation/wish fulfillment
- Depersonalization as defense
- Memory reconstruction

**Problems with Psychological Theories**:
- Occur in children without death concept
- Cross-cultural consistency
- Transformative power greater than dreams

**Non-Materialist Theories**:
- Consciousness not produced by brain
- Filter/transmission model
- Survival of consciousness after death

### 5.2 Terminal Lucidity

#### Definition
Unexpected return of mental clarity and memory shortly before death, particularly in patients with severe cognitive impairment from dementia, brain tumors, or strokes.

#### Case Features

**Classic Presentation**:
- Patient with severe dementia, non-communicative for months/years
- Hours to days before death
- Suddenly recognizes family members
- Speaks coherently about past
- Shows personality characteristics thought lost
- Brief period (minutes to hours)
- Followed by decline and death

**Historical Documentation**:
- Described since antiquity
- Benjamin Rush (1812): Early medical description
- Observed by hospice workers worldwide

**Modern Research**:
- Nahm & Greyson (2009): Systematic literature review
- Nahm et al. (2012): Survey study
- Macleod (2009): Hospice observations

#### Theoretical Significance

**Challenge to Localization**:
- Memories presumed "erased" by Alzheimer's somehow accessed
- Personality presumed destroyed somehow reconstituted
- Brain damage irreversible yet function returns

**Possible Explanations**:

*Neurobiological*:
- Surge of stress hormones/neuromodulators
- Release from pathological inhibition
- Network reconfiguration in dying brain

*Non-Materialist*:
- Brain as filter for consciousness
- Dying brain becomes "more transparent"
- Mind-brain relationship not what we assume

### 5.3 Consciousness in the Dying Process

#### Physiological Changes

**Active Dying** (hours to days):
- Decreased blood pressure and perfusion
- Altered breathing patterns
- Reduced kidney function
- Changes in consciousness

**Near Death** (hours):
- Cheyne-Stokes breathing
- Terminal restlessness or calmness
- Decreased responsiveness
- Mottling of skin

**Death** (minutes):
- Cessation of heartbeat
- Cessation of breathing
- Brain death follows (minutes without oxygen)

#### Consciousness Features

**Nearing Death Awareness** (Callanan & Kelley, 1992):
- Seeing deceased relatives
- Describing "going somewhere"
- Symbolic language about journeys
- Awareness of death timing

**Deathbed Visions**:
- Reported across cultures
- Typically see deceased relatives
- Different from hallucinations (coherent, purposeful)
- Often comforting

**Hearing at Death**:
- Evidence hearing persists longest
- Hospice practice: assume they can hear
- Final sense to leave

### 5.4 Hospice Observations

**What Hospice Workers Report**:
- Pattern of pre-death experiences
- "Rally" before death in some patients
- Timing of death (waiting for family, or waiting for privacy)
- Peaceful deaths more common than feared

**Implications for Consciousness**:
- End-of-life consciousness may be richer than assumed
- Person still "present" to varying degrees
- Quality of dying matters
- Consciousness may transform rather than simply end

            gestational_weeks: For prenatal queries

        Returns:
            Appropriate DevelopmentalStage

        pass
