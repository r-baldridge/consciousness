Standard Procedure (step‑by‑step)

Step Action Details / Tips

1. Scheduling

Usually once a week (Rinzai) or as needed
(Sōtō). The student signs up at the front desk or informs the abbot.
Arrive

minutes early; punctuality is a sign of respect.

2. Preparation

 Review your practice log
(zazen minutes, koan notes, any difficulties).  Formulate a concise question or a brief description of the current obstacle.
## Keep the question short

(one sentence). The teacher often asks, What is happening now? rather than a long exposition.

3. Entering the room

Bow at the entrance, place your shoes neatly, and sit on the cushion facing the teachers low seat (or directly opposite if the room is very small).
Maintain the five‑posture model
(head, spine, shoulders, hands, legs).

4. Opening greeting

Traditional greeting: Namu

Myōhō

Renge‑Kyō
(or a simple Good morning, teacher). The teacher replies with a nod or a brief phrase.
The greeting establishes sangha etiquette and signals the start of the formal interaction.

5. Presentation of the issue

Speak clearly, calmly, and directly: I have been practicing the Mu koan for three weeks, but each time I sit I become overwhelmed by thoughts of work.
Avoid rambling; the teachers time is limited. If you have a diary entry, you may hand it over after speaking.

6. Teachers response

The teacher may:  Ask a probing question
(What does the word Mu mean to you right now?).  Offer a direct pointing
(When you hear Mu, let the sound dissolve into the space of your breath.).  Assign a new koan or adjust your posture/breath.
The response can be a silence; in Rinzai a prolonged stare is a powerful pointing.

7. Students reflection

After the teachers remark, remain silent for a few breaths, allowing the guidance to settle. If clarification is needed, ask politely (Could you elaborate on the space of breath?).
The key is non‑reactive
the insight often arises in the pause.

8. Closing

Bow again, thank the teacher, and exit quietly. Some lineages require a final bow to the shoji
(sliding door) as a symbolic departure from the teaching space.
Take a moment to integrate the guidance before returning to the meditation hall.

9. Post‑dokusan practice

Apply the teachers suggestion immediately in the next zazen or koan session. Write a brief note in your practice log: Teacher suggested focusing on breath‑space with Mu.
Consistency is crucial; the teachers pointer is a seed that needs nurturing.

Psychological & Phenomenological Mechanisms

Mechanism How it works in dokusan Why it matters for liberation

## Authority‑guided upāya (skillful means)

The teachers pointed comment bypasses the students conceptual analysis, delivering a direct experiential cue.
Cuts through the discursive loop that sustains avidyā (ignorance).

Mirror‑neuron resonance

The student observes the teachers calm demeanor, posture, and tone; the brains mirror system mirrors these qualities, fostering karuṇā and prajñā.
Reinforces the bodhisattva aspiration to embody compassion.

Feedback loop

Immediate, specific feedback corrects maladaptive habits (e.g., slouching, shallow breathing).
Reduces physical tension that can become a barrier to shikantaza and kenshō.

Social‑cognitive validation

Confirmation of a kenshō or koan resolution validates the students experience, strengthening confidence in the path.
Encourages continued diligent practice, preventing discouragement.

Containment of anxiety

The structured, time‑limited setting provides a safe container for confronting difficult emotions that arise during practice.
Allows the practitioner to face kleshas
(afflictive emotions) without being overwhelmed.

Path from Dokusan to Liberation Preparation
 The student arrives with a clear, honest appraisal of the current obstacle.
Encounter
 The teachers direct pointing creates a cognitive rupture
(the minds habitual narrative is interrupted).
## Gap (kenshō‑like moment)

 In the pause after the teachers remark, a spontaneous insight often surfaces: the mind sees its own empty nature.
Integration
 The student applies the guidance in subsequent zazen, koan work, or daily life, gradually refining the mind‑body alignment.
Deepening
 Repeated dokusan sessions accelerate the purification of subtle defilements, making the mind increasingly receptive to silent illumination.
## Full liberation

 When the student no longer depends on external pointers because the mind naturally rests in its luminous emptiness, the dokusan becomes a formal acknowledgment rather than a necessity; the practitioner lives continuously in the awakened state.

Common Pitfalls & How to Correct Them

Pitfall Why it occurs Remedy

Over‑intellectualizing the teachers words

The mind tries to solve the guidance analytically.

Label the thought (analyzing) and return to the breath; treat the teachers remark as a pointer, not a doctrine.

## Fear of failure

 worrying about not having a breakthrough during dokusan.
Attachment to outcomes (desire for kenshō).

Remind yourself that dokusan is a process, not a test. The teachers role is to guide, not to judge.

## Speaking too much

 giving a long life story before the core issue.
Anxiety about wasting the teachers time.

Prepare a one‑sentence summary beforehand; practice delivering it aloud before the session.

## Physical stiffness

 sitting too rigidly, causing discomfort.
Lack of prior posture work.

Do a brief body‑scan before entering; adjust cushion height to ensure a relaxed spine.

## Misreading silence

 assuming a silent stare means disapproval.
## Cultural unfamiliarity.

In Rinzai, silence can be the most potent pointing; stay present and let the stillness speak to you.

Clinging to the teachers blessing
 treating the teachers approval as the ultimate goal.
Ego‑reinforcement.

Label the feeling (pride) and let it pass; remember the teaching is a means to see your own mind.

Skipping post‑dokusan practice
 waiting days before applying the guidance.
Procrastination or lack of discipline.

Schedule a short zazen immediately after dokusan (5

min) to test the teachers suggestion while the impression is fresh.
