# Advaita Vedanta: The Non-Dual Tradition

## 1. Overview and Definition

Advaita Vedanta (Sanskrit: *advaita* -- "not two"; *vedanta* -- "end of the Vedas") is the most influential school of non-dual philosophy within the Hindu intellectual tradition. Its central claim is deceptively simple and radically far-reaching: there is only one reality, and that reality is Brahman -- pure, infinite, undivided consciousness. The individual self (*Atman*) is not separate from this ultimate reality; it *is* Brahman. The apparent multiplicity of the world -- the diversity of objects, persons, and experiences -- is not ultimately real but arises from *maya* (cosmic illusion) and *avidya* (individual ignorance).

The tradition asserts that liberation (*moksha*) does not consist of going somewhere or acquiring something new. It is the direct recognition of what has always been the case: that one's own innermost awareness is identical with the absolute ground of all existence. The famous Upanishadic declaration *Tat tvam asi* ("That thou art") encapsulates the entire teaching in three words.

Advaita Vedanta is not merely a metaphysical system. It is a soteriological path -- a practical framework designed to dissolve the illusion of separateness and reveal the non-dual nature of consciousness. As such, it sits at the intersection of philosophy, contemplative practice, and what we might now call consciousness studies.

## 2. Historical Origins

### Vedic and Upanishadic Roots

The philosophical foundation of Advaita Vedanta lies in the Upanishads, the concluding portions of the Vedas composed between approximately 800 and 200 BCE. These texts represent a decisive turn from the ritualistic concerns of earlier Vedic literature toward questions about the ultimate nature of the self and reality.

Three Upanishads are of particular importance for the Advaita tradition:

- **Chandogya Upanishad** -- Contains the teaching of Uddalaka Aruni to his son Shvetaketu, culminating in the repeated refrain *Tat tvam asi* ("That thou art"), identifying the individual self with the universal ground.
- **Brihadaranyaka Upanishad** -- The largest and arguably oldest of the principal Upanishads. Its dialogues, particularly those involving the sage Yajnavalkya, introduce the method of *neti neti* ("not this, not this") -- defining Brahman by negating everything that Brahman is not.
- **Mandukya Upanishad** -- Though the shortest of the principal Upanishads (only twelve verses), it became the foundation for Gaudapada's systematic non-dual philosophy. It analyzes the four states of consciousness -- waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and *turiya* (the "fourth," which is pure awareness itself) -- and equates these with the syllable *AUM*.

### The Brahma Sutras

The *Brahma Sutras* (also called *Vedanta Sutras*), attributed to Badarayana (c. 4th century BCE to 2nd century CE), attempted to systematize the diverse and sometimes contradictory teachings of the Upanishads into a coherent philosophical framework. These terse aphorisms became the subject of extensive commentarial traditions. Shankara's commentary on the Brahma Sutras remains the definitive Advaita interpretation, though rival Vedantic schools -- notably Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita and Madhva's Dvaita -- offer fundamentally different readings of the same text.

Together, the Upanishads, the Brahma Sutras, and the Bhagavad Gita form the *Prasthanatrayi* ("triple canon") that every Vedantic school is expected to interpret coherently.

## 3. Key Figures

### Gaudapada (c. 6th century CE)

The earliest known systematic exponent of Advaita. His *Mandukya Karika* (also called *Agama Shastra*) is a metrical commentary on the Mandukya Upanishad that introduces the doctrine of *ajativada* -- the thesis that nothing has ever been born or created, and that the appearance of origination is itself illusory. Gaudapada drew parallels with Buddhist Madhyamaka philosophy, particularly the thought of Nagarjuna, a connection that has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate. He is traditionally regarded as the grand-teacher (*paramaguru*) of Shankara.

### Adi Shankara (788-820 CE)

The towering figure of the tradition. In a life of remarkable brevity -- traditionally held to span only thirty-two years -- Shankara composed commentaries (*bhashyas*) on the principal Upanishads, the Brahma Sutras, and the Bhagavad Gita, thereby establishing the definitive Advaita reading of the entire Prasthanatrayi. He formulated the theory of *adhyasa* (superimposition), arguing in the introduction to his Brahma Sutra Bhashya that all worldly experience rests on the fundamental error of confusing the self with the not-self. Shankara also founded four monastic centers (*mathas*) at the cardinal points of the Indian subcontinent -- Sringeri, Dvaraka, Puri, and Jyotirmath -- which continue to function as institutional centers of Advaita teaching to this day.

### Suresvara (8th-9th century CE)

A direct disciple of Shankara, Suresvara authored the *Naishkarmya Siddhi* ("Attainment of Actionlessness"), an independent work defending the view that liberation arises from knowledge alone, not from any combination of knowledge and action (*jnana-karma-samuccaya*). He also wrote extensive sub-commentaries (*vartikas*) on Shankara's commentaries on the Brihadaranyaka and Taittiriya Upanishads.

### Padmapada (8th-9th century CE)

Another direct disciple of Shankara, Padmapada composed the *Panchapadika*, a commentary on the first five sections of Shankara's Brahma Sutra Bhashya. This work became the basis for the later Vivarana sub-school of Advaita.

### Vacaspati Misra (9th-10th century CE)

Author of the *Bhamati*, a commentary on Shankara's Brahma Sutra Bhashya that founded the Bhamati sub-school. Vacaspati's interpretation differs from the Vivarana school on several technical points, most notably on the locus and object of *avidya* (ignorance) and the nature of the individual self's relationship to Brahman prior to liberation.

### Prakasatman (10th century CE)

Composed the *Panchapadika-Vivarana*, a commentary on Padmapada's Panchapadika, which established the Vivarana sub-school as a distinct interpretive tradition within Advaita. The Vivarana school holds that *avidya* resides in Brahman itself (rather than in the individual self, as the Bhamati school maintains) and emphasizes the self-luminous nature of consciousness.

### Vidyaranya (14th century CE)

A pivotal figure in the late medieval revival of Advaita, Vidyaranya authored the *Panchadasi*, a widely studied introductory text that presents the core Advaita teaching through systematic analysis, analogy, and meditative instruction. His *Jivanmuktiviveka* ("Discrimination of Living Liberation") offers an extended treatment of the concept that liberation can be attained while still embodied -- the *jivanmukta* ideal.

### Madhusudana Sarasvati (16th century CE)

Composed the *Advaita Siddhi*, a rigorous dialectical defense of Advaita against the formidable objections raised by the Dvaita (dualist) school of Vyasatirtha. The work represents one of the most sophisticated exercises in Indian philosophical debate and demonstrates the logical rigor that the Advaita tradition could bring to bear on its own metaphysical claims.

### Ramana Maharshi (1879-1950)

A modern sage who experienced a spontaneous awakening at the age of sixteen and spent the remainder of his life at Arunachala in Tamil Nadu. Ramana is best known for the practice of *atma vichara* ("self-inquiry"), centered on the question "Who am I?" He taught that sustained inquiry into the source of the "I"-thought leads directly to the recognition of the self as pure awareness. Though not a traditional Advaitin in the scholastic sense, Ramana's teaching is widely regarded as a distillation of the Advaita insight into its most immediate, practice-oriented form.

### Nisargadatta Maharaj (1897-1981)

A householder and shopkeeper in Mumbai who became one of the most widely read non-dual teachers of the twentieth century through the publication of *I Am That* (1973), a collection of his dialogues. Nisargadatta's teaching centers on abiding in the sense of "I am" -- the bare feeling of existence prior to all conceptual elaboration -- as a direct path to the recognition of one's identity with the absolute.

## 4. Core Philosophical Claims

### Brahman as the Sole Reality

Advaita holds that Brahman is the only reality (*sat*) and defines it through the formula *sat-chit-ananda*: existence (*sat*), consciousness (*chit*), and bliss (*ananda*). These are not three attributes that Brahman possesses; rather, they are three ways of pointing at its single, indivisible nature. Brahman is not a being among beings, not a supreme deity set above the world, but the very ground of being, awareness, and value.

### Three Levels of Reality

Advaita distinguishes three orders of reality:

- **Paramarthika** (absolute reality) -- Brahman alone, non-dual, unchanging, self-luminous.
- **Vyavaharika** (empirical reality) -- The everyday world of persons, objects, causal relations, and moral law. This level is real *within its own frame of reference* but is ultimately sublated by the recognition of Brahman.
- **Pratibhasika** (apparent reality) -- Illusory appearances such as the rope mistaken for a snake, or the water seen in a desert mirage. These are false even within the empirical frame.

This three-tiered ontology allows Advaita to take the phenomenal world seriously at the practical level (ethics, worship, and social life all operate within *vyavaharika*) while maintaining that the ultimate truth is non-dual.

### Maya and Avidya

*Maya* is the power by which Brahman appears as the manifold world without undergoing any real change. It is said to be *anirvachaniya* -- neither real nor unreal, and therefore logically indeterminate. *Avidya* (ignorance) is the individual expression of this cosmic power: it is the beginningless misapprehension that takes the self to be a limited, embodied agent rather than infinite Brahman.

### The Mahavakyas (Great Sayings)

Four utterances from the Upanishads are singled out as the definitive expressions of the non-dual teaching:

| Mahavakya | Source | Translation |
|---|---|---|
| *Prajnanam Brahma* | Aitareya Upanishad | "Consciousness is Brahman" |
| *Aham Brahmasmi* | Brihadaranyaka Upanishad | "I am Brahman" |
| *Tat tvam asi* | Chandogya Upanishad | "That thou art" |
| *Ayam Atma Brahma* | Mandukya Upanishad | "This self is Brahman" |

Each mahavakya is traditionally associated with one of the four Vedas and one of the four monastic orders founded by Shankara.

### Superimposition (Adhyasa)

Shankara's Brahma Sutra Bhashya opens with the *Adhyasa Bhashya*, which argues that all worldly experience is founded on a mutual superimposition (*adhyasa*) of the self and the not-self. We project attributes of the body-mind complex onto the self (mistaking ourselves for limited beings) and simultaneously project the self's qualities of consciousness and existence onto inert matter. This fundamental confusion is the root of *samsara* (the cycle of birth and death), and its removal through knowledge is liberation.

## 5. Practices and Methods

### The Three-Fold Discipline

The classical Advaita path unfolds in three stages:

1. **Shravana** (hearing) -- Listening to the teaching from a qualified teacher (*guru*) who transmits the meaning of the Upanishadic texts, particularly the mahavakyas. This is not passive reception but an engaged process of understanding the intended meaning of the texts.
2. **Manana** (reflection) -- Rational examination of the teaching to resolve doubts and eliminate apparent contradictions. This stage involves philosophical reasoning and debate.
3. **Nididhyasana** (deep meditation) -- Sustained contemplative absorption on the truth that has been heard and reflected upon, until it becomes direct, unmediated realization rather than mere intellectual assent.

### Atma Vichara (Self-Inquiry)

Popularized by Ramana Maharshi, self-inquiry involves tracing every thought and experience back to its source by asking "Who am I?" or "To whom does this thought arise?" The method does not seek an answer in words but rather the dissolution of the questioner into the pure awareness that is the ground of the "I"-thought.

### Neti Neti (Not This, Not This)

The *via negativa* of Advaita, drawn from the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad. The practitioner systematically negates everything that can be objectified -- body, mind, thoughts, emotions, perceptions -- recognizing that the self cannot be any object of awareness because it is awareness itself. What remains when all objects have been negated is Brahman.

### The Four Prerequisites (Sadhana Chatushtaya)

Shankara specifies four qualifications that a student must cultivate before undertaking the serious study of Vedanta:

1. **Viveka** -- Discrimination between the eternal (Brahman) and the non-eternal (everything else).
2. **Vairagya** -- Dispassion toward the enjoyment of results, whether in this world or any other.
3. **Shat-sampatti** -- The six virtues: *shama* (mental tranquility), *dama* (sense control), *uparati* (withdrawal), *titiksha* (endurance), *shraddha* (faith in the teaching and teacher), and *samadhana* (single-pointed focus).
4. **Mumukshutva** -- An intense longing for liberation.

### Study with a Qualified Teacher

Advaita emphasizes that the teaching must be received from a teacher (*guru*) who has both thorough knowledge of the scriptures (*shrotriya*) and direct realization of Brahman (*brahmanishtha*). The guru does not transmit something the student lacks; rather, the guru removes the ignorance that conceals what the student already is.

## 6. Primary Texts

### The Prasthanatrayi (Triple Canon)

- **The Principal Upanishads** -- Especially the Chandogya, Brihadaranyaka, Mandukya, Aitareya, Taittiriya, Kena, Isha, Katha, Prashna, and Mundaka. These are the *shruti prasthana* (revealed scripture foundation).
- **The Brahma Sutras** of Badarayana -- The *nyaya prasthana* (logical foundation), consisting of 555 aphorisms that systematize Upanishadic teaching.
- **The Bhagavad Gita** -- The *smriti prasthana* (traditional scripture foundation), particularly chapters 2, 13, and 15 on the nature of the self and the distinction between the field and the knower of the field.

### Other Major Works

- **Vivekachudamani** ("Crest-Jewel of Discrimination") -- Attributed to Shankara, a verse work that presents the Advaita path from initial disillusionment to final realization.
- **Upadesa Sahasri** ("A Thousand Teachings") -- Shankara's only independent (non-commentarial) prose-and-verse work, offering direct instruction in the non-dual teaching.
- **Aparokshanubhuti** ("Self-Realization") -- Attributed to Shankara, a concise text on direct experiential knowledge of the self.
- **Panchadasi** of Vidyaranya -- A systematic fifteen-chapter treatment covering discrimination, illumination, and bliss.
- **Naishkarmya Siddhi** of Suresvara -- A defense of knowledge as the sole means to liberation.
- **Advaita Siddhi** of Madhusudana Sarasvati -- A dialectical masterwork addressing all major objections to Advaita.

## 7. Relationship to Consciousness Studies

Advaita Vedanta anticipates and intersects with several problems in contemporary consciousness research:

### The Hard Problem

David Chalmers' "hard problem of consciousness" -- explaining why and how subjective experience arises from physical processes -- finds a striking analogue in the Advaita framework. Advaita's answer is that the hard problem is insoluble on its own terms because it assumes that consciousness arises from matter, which Advaita regards as a fundamental category error. Consciousness (*chit*) does not arise; it is the foundational reality from which the appearance of matter is projected. The hard problem dissolves not by being solved but by reversing the explanatory priority: matter is *in* consciousness, not the other way around.

### Awareness as Fundamental

The Advaita claim that consciousness is *svayam-prakasha* (self-luminous) -- that awareness reveals itself and needs no external illumination -- resonates with the position in contemporary philosophy of mind known as "consciousness-first" or "awareness-first" approaches. The phenomenological tradition (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty) and certain interpretive frameworks in quantum mechanics (Wheeler's "participatory universe," the von Neumann-Wigner interpretation) share the intuition that consciousness cannot be derived from non-conscious constituents.

### Connections to Panpsychism

Modern panpsychism -- the view that mentality or experiential quality is a fundamental feature of the natural world -- shares structural similarities with Advaita's claim that Brahman (consciousness) is the ground of all phenomena. However, a crucial distinction remains: panpsychism typically distributes micro-consciousness across all physical entities, whereas Advaita posits a single, undivided consciousness that merely *appears* as multiple centers of experience due to *maya*. Advaita is thus closer to cosmopsychism (the view that there is one cosmic consciousness) than to standard panpsychism.

### States of Consciousness and Turiya

The Mandukya Upanishad's analysis of waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and *turiya* (the "fourth") provides a contemplative phenomenology of consciousness states that parallels modern sleep and meditation research. The recognition that awareness persists through all three ordinary states -- and that there is a "witness" awareness that underlies them -- anticipates findings in lucid dreaming research, meditation neuroscience, and the study of awareness under anesthesia.

## 8. Connection to Other Non-Dual Traditions

### Advaita and Buddhism: Madhyamaka and Yogacara

The relationship between Advaita Vedanta and Buddhist philosophy -- particularly Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka ("Middle Way") -- is one of the most debated topics in Indian intellectual history. Both traditions deny the ultimate reality of the phenomenal world as ordinarily perceived. Both employ rigorous dialectical methods to dismantle naive realism.

However, the traditions diverge on a critical point. Madhyamaka holds that *all* phenomena, including any posited ultimate reality, are empty (*shunya*) of inherent existence. There is no underlying substrate. Advaita, by contrast, insists on a positive, self-luminous ground: Brahman is not emptiness but fullness (*purna*). The Advaita critique of Buddhism is that *shunyata* collapses into nihilism if there is no conscious witness in which emptiness is recognized. The Buddhist rejoinder is that positing Brahman reintroduces the very substantialism that rigorous analysis is meant to dissolve.

Gaudapada's *Mandukya Karika* occupies a unique position in this debate, borrowing extensively from Madhyamaka and Yogacara terminology while grounding its conclusions in Upanishadic authority.

### Advaita and Kashmir Shaivism

Kashmir Shaivism, particularly the Pratyabhijna ("Recognition") school of Utpaladeva and Abhinavagupta (9th-11th centuries), offers a non-dual teaching that differs from Advaita in important respects. While both hold that consciousness is ultimate, Kashmir Shaivism identifies this consciousness with Shiva as a dynamic, creative principle (*svatantrya*, absolute freedom). The world is not *maya* in the Advaita sense of an illusory superimposition; it is a real expression (*abhasa*) of Shiva's creative power (*shakti*). Kashmir Shaivism thus maintains non-duality while affirming the reality of the world as consciousness in its self-expressive mode -- a position sometimes described as "realistic idealism" in contrast to Advaita's "illusionistic idealism."

### Advaita and Zen Buddhism

Zen and Advaita share a commitment to direct, non-conceptual realization and a suspicion of purely intellectual approaches to liberation. Both traditions emphasize the role of a qualified teacher, the inadequacy of words, and the priority of immediate experience. Ramana Maharshi's *atma vichara* ("Who am I?") bears a structural resemblance to Zen koan practice insofar as both employ a question not to obtain a conceptual answer but to exhaust the conceptual mind and reveal what lies beneath it.

The key difference lies in their metaphysical commitments. Zen, as a Buddhist tradition, operates within the framework of *shunyata* (emptiness) and *pratityasamutpada* (dependent origination), denying any permanent self or substrate. Advaita posits the Atman -- an unchanging, self-luminous awareness -- as the very reality that Zen claims to be empty. Whether this difference is substantive or merely terminological -- whether "emptiness" and "Brahman" are two fingers pointing at the same moon -- remains one of the most fertile questions in comparative philosophy of consciousness.
