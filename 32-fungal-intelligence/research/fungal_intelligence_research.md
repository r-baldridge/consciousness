## 1. Scientific Foundation

### 1.1 Key Researchers

#### Paul Stamets (b. 1955)
Paul Edward Stamets is an American mycologist, entrepreneur, and author who has spent more than 40 years studying mushrooms and the intricate underground fungal networks called mycelium. He is the founder of Fungi Perfecti and author of seminal works including *Mycelium Running: How Mushrooms Can Help Save the World*.

**Key Contributions:**
- Coined the concept of mycelium as "Earth's natural internet"
- Demonstrated that mycelia transmit information using neurotransmitters similar to those in human brains
- Pioneered mycoremediation techniques for environmental cleanup
- Discovered antiviral compounds in fungi that protect honeybees from colony collapse disorder
- Proposed that mycelial networks possess purposeful intelligence

**Notable Quote:** "The mycelium grows literally millions of end-tips in a cubic centimeter that then encounter new food, toxins, new opportunities, new challenges. It's like having little scientific teams at the end of every little tip of mycelium that are working together to see if they can come up with a solution."

#### Suzanne Simard
Suzanne Simard is a Canadian forest ecologist and Professor at the University of British Columbia, recognized as one of TIME Magazine's 100 most influential people in the world for 2024. She pioneered research on the "Wood Wide Web" and the concept of "Mother Trees."

**Key Contributions:**
- Discovered that trees communicate and share resources through underground mycorrhizal networks
- Identified "hub trees" or "mother trees" as central nodes in forest communication networks
- Demonstrated that Douglas firs preferentially provide carbon to their own offspring over unrelated seedlings
- Found that mother trees modify their root structure to accommodate baby trees
- Established the Mother Tree Project (2015) at UBC for regenerative forestry research

**Recent Publications (2024-2025):**
- Thomson, Roach, Miller, and Simard (2025): "The Interactive Role of Climatic Transfer Distance and Overstory Retention on Douglas-Fir Seedling Survival and Height Growth in Interior British Columbia" in *Global Change Biology*
- Simard, Ryan, and Perry (2025): Response paper addressing questions about common mycorrhizal networks in *Frontiers in Forests and Global Change*

#### Merlin Sheldrake
Merlin Sheldrake is a British biologist specializing in mycorrhizal fungi, holding a PhD from the University of Cambridge. He is an advisor to the Society for the Protection of Underground Networks (SPUN) and author of the Royal Society Prize-winning book *Entangled Life* (2020).

**Key Contributions:**
- Explored how fungi challenge our understanding of non-human intelligence
- Documented how fungi stretch traditional definitions of biological individuality
- Investigated psilocybin production and the evolutionary role of fungi
- Advanced public understanding of the interconnectedness of all life through fungal networks

**Research Areas:**
- Fungal biology and the history of Amazonian ethnobotany
- Tropical ecology and underground fungal networks in Panama
- Philosophy of biological individuality and intelligence

### 1.2 Mycorrhizal Network Discoveries

#### Common Mycorrhizal Networks (CMNs)
Research over the past five decades using increasingly sophisticated tools (microscopy, DNA sequencing, microsatellites, and isotopic tracing) has revealed that CMNs connect the roots of trees with other trees, seedlings, shrubs, and mycoheterotrophic plants.

**Key Findings:**
- CMNs facilitate nutrient, carbon, water, and infochemical transfer among trees
- Multiple belowground pathways function simultaneously: CMNs, mycorrhizal roots, and soil
- Resource transfer is affected by light, water, nutrient, and health status of donor and recipient trees
- Fungal species characteristics in the CMN influence transfer rates
- Networks appear as early as 460 million years ago (arbuscular mycorrhizae)

#### Network Functionality
- **Carbon Transfer:** Trees can share up to 40% of their carbon with neighboring trees through mycorrhizal networks
- **Stress Signaling:** Trees can send chemical signals through networks to warn neighbors of pest attacks
- **Preferential Allocation:** Mother trees preferentially allocate resources to kin seedlings
- **Seasonal Dynamics:** Resource flow direction changes based on seasonal needs and tree health

### 1.3 Physarum polycephalum (Slime Mold) Intelligence

#### Background
Physarum polycephalum is an acellular slime mold—a single giant cell with millions of nuclei. Despite lacking a brain or nervous system, it demonstrates remarkable cognitive abilities including sensing, communication, navigation, decision-making, memory, and learning.

#### Cognitive Capabilities Demonstrated:
1. **Maze Solving:** In 2000, Toshiyuki Nakagaki's team showed P. polycephalum could find the shortest path through a maze (Ig Nobel Prize 2008)
2. **Network Optimization:** Creates efficient transport networks comparable to human infrastructure
3. **Habituation Learning:** Learns to ignore repeated stimuli (quinine, caffeine) and recovers responsiveness when stimulus is removed
4. **Memory Storage:** Stores memories in tube diameter variations within its network structure
5. **Memory Transfer:** Can transfer learned information from one individual to another through fusion
6. **Multi-Objective Optimization:** Balances efficiency, fault tolerance, and cost in network design

### 1.4 Tokyo Rail Network Recreation

#### The Experiment (2010)
Researchers led by Toshiyuki Nakagaki at Hokkaido University conducted a landmark experiment demonstrating slime mold's network optimization abilities.

**Methodology:**
1. Placed oat flakes (a P. polycephalum favorite) on agar plates in positions mimicking 36 cities around Tokyo
2. Positioned P. polycephalum at the point representing Tokyo
3. Observed network formation over 26 hours

**Results:**
- The slime mold created networks with comparable efficiency, fault tolerance, and cost to the actual Tokyo rail system
- Different plates exhibited various solutions, but many showed striking visual similarity to actual rail networks
- Where slime mold chose different routes, alternatives were equally or more efficient

**Publication:** Tero et al. (2010), "Rules for Biologically Inspired Adaptive Network Design," *Science* 327:439

**Subsequent Applications:**
- Mexican highway network approximation
- Iberian highway system modeling
- US Route 20 optimization
- German Autobahn 7 analysis

### 1.5 Fungal Memory and Learning

#### Types of Memory Demonstrated

**Short-Term Memory:**
- P. polycephalum can remember and anticipate periodic events
- Maintains temporal patterns even after stimulus removal
- Duration: hours to days

**Long-Term Memory:**
- Slime molds habituated to sodium (a repellent) retained this learning through dormancy
- Memory preserved for over one month in dormant sclerotia state
- Chemical analyses showed continuous uptake and retention of sodium during habituation

**Memory Mechanism:**
- Research suggests slime molds use "circulating memory" through absorbed substances
- Tube diameter variations encode spatial memories of explored areas
- Memory inception can be induced by constrained absorption (2 hours exposure sufficient)

#### Learning Types
1. **Habituation:** Decreasing response to repeated stimuli
2. **Sensitization:** Increased response after exposure to strong stimuli
3. **Associative Learning:** Evidence suggests basic conditioning capabilities
4. **Spatial Learning:** Remembers previously explored areas to avoid revisiting

## 2. Intelligence Domains

### 2.1 Network Topology Optimization
**Description:** Fungi demonstrate sophisticated abilities to create efficient network structures that minimize resource use while maximizing connectivity.

**Mechanisms:**
- Tubes carrying high nutrient volume expand; underused tubes contract and disappear
- Network constantly remodels based on environmental feedback
- Achieves near-optimal solutions to NP-hard problems (Steiner tree, traveling salesman)

**Applications:**
- Transportation network design
- Communication network optimization
- Supply chain logistics

### 2.2 Resource Allocation Decisions
**Description:** Mycorrhizal networks make complex decisions about where to allocate resources across connected organisms.

**Evidence:**
- Trees share carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water through networks
- Allocation is not random but follows patterns suggesting decision-making
- Resources flow preferentially to seedlings, stressed trees, and kin
- Source-sink dynamics respond to environmental conditions

### 2.3 Chemical Communication
**Description:** Fungi communicate through an extensive vocabulary of chemical signals including volatile organic compounds (VOCs).

**VOC Characteristics:**
- Over 300 distinct VOCs identified from fungi
- Include alcohols, aldehydes, acids, ethers, esters, ketones, terpenes
- 1-octen-3-ol ("mushroom alcohol") is most common fungal volatile
- Can travel through air and soil over long distances

**Communication Functions:**
- Species recognition
- Mating signals
- Defense coordination
- Nutrient availability indication
- Inter-kingdom communication with plants and animals

### 2.4 Electrical Signaling
**Description:** Fungi generate and transmit electrical signals similar to neural action potentials.

**Characteristics:**
- Spike duration: 1 to 21 hours
- Amplitude: 0.03 to 2.1 mV
- Species-specific spiking patterns
- Spikes often cluster into trains

**Research Findings:**
- Oyster fungi (*Pleurotus djamor*) generate action potential-like spikes
- Adamatzky (2022) proposed spike trains could encode up to 50 "words"
- Electrical bursts originate in specific directions and recruit other regions
- Propagation delays range from seconds to hours

**Debate:**
- Some researchers question whether signals represent true communication
- Concerns about distinguishing biological signals from voltage fluctuations

### 2.5 Memory Traces in Mycelium
**Description:** Mycelial networks store information about past experiences in their physical structure.

**Mechanisms:**
- Tube diameter encodes frequency of use
- Chemical gradients mark explored territories
- Network architecture reflects environmental history
- Dormant forms preserve learned information

### 2.6 Problem-Solving Capabilities
**Description:** Fungi solve complex computational problems without centralized control.

**Demonstrated Problem Types:**
- Shortest path finding
- Network flow optimization
- Multi-objective optimization (efficiency vs. robustness)
- Risk assessment and avoidance

### 2.7 Symbiotic Negotiation
**Description:** Mycorrhizal fungi engage in sophisticated exchange relationships with host plants.

**Exchange Dynamics:**
- Fungi provide: phosphorus, nitrogen, water, trace minerals, disease protection
- Plants provide: carbon (sugars from photosynthesis)
- Exchange rates vary based on market-like conditions
- "Cheater" detection and punishment mechanisms exist

### 2.8 Stress Response and Adaptation
**Description:** Fungi demonstrate rapid physiological and behavioral responses to environmental stress.

**Response Types:**
- Heat shock protein production
- Melanin synthesis for radiation protection
- Hyphal branching pattern modification
- Dormancy induction

### 2.9 Collective Decision-Making
**Description:** Fungal networks make unified decisions despite lacking central control.

**Examples:**
- Directional growth choices
- Resource allocation priorities
- Sporulation timing
- Symbiont selection

### 2.10 Temporal Anticipation
**Description:** Some fungi can anticipate periodic events and prepare in advance.

**Evidence:**
- P. polycephalum anticipates regular feeding times
- Circadian rhythm regulation of bioluminescence
- Seasonal sporulation timing

### 2.11 Spatial Navigation
**Description:** Fungi navigate complex three-dimensional environments effectively.

**Mechanisms:**
- Chemotaxis (movement toward/away from chemicals)
- Galvanotropism (response to electrical fields)
- Gravitropism (response to gravity)
- Thigmotropism (response to touch/surfaces)

### 2.12 Self-Recognition and Fusion
**Description:** Fungi can distinguish self from non-self and selectively fuse with compatible individuals.

**het Genes:**
- Vegetative incompatibility genes control fusion decisions
- Prevent exploitation by genetically distinct individuals
- Allow resource sharing within clonal networks

### 2.13 Predatory Behavior
**Description:** Some fungi actively hunt and capture prey using sophisticated mechanisms.

**Examples:**
- Nematode-trapping fungi (*Arthrobotrys*) create adhesive nets, constricting rings
- Detection of prey through chemical and mechanical cues
- Rapid ring constriction (0.1 seconds)

### 2.14 Behavioral Manipulation of Hosts
**Description:** Parasitic fungi alter host behavior with remarkable precision.

**Example: Ophiocordyceps unilateralis (Zombie Ant Fungus)**
- Causes infected ants to leave nests at unusual times
- Induces climbing to optimal height (26 cm)
- Forces "death grip" on leaf vein
- Creates ideal conditions for spore dispersal

**Mechanism:**
- Fungal cells infect muscle fibers directly
- Bioactive compounds (sphingosine, GBA) affect nervous system
- Brain remains intact; control is peripheral

### 2.15 Information Integration
**Description:** Fungal networks integrate multiple information streams to make adaptive decisions.

**Integrated Signals:**
- Chemical gradients
- Electrical potentials
- Mechanical forces
- Light levels
- Temperature

### 2.16 Wound Response and Healing
**Description:** Fungi actively repair damage to their networks.

**Mechanisms:**
- Rapid sealing of damaged hyphae
- Rerouting of resources around damaged areas
- Regeneration of lost structures
- Scar tissue formation

## 3. Fungal Types Coverage

### 3.1 Mycorrhizal Species

#### 3.1.1 Ectomycorrhizal Fungi
**Characteristics:**
- Form a "Hartig net" between root cells (do not penetrate cells)
- Create a hyphal sheath (mantle) around root tips
- Over 5,000 species identified
- Account for approximately 3% of mycorrhizal associations

**Associated Plants:**
- Conifers (Pinus, Picea, Abies)
- Deciduous trees: Fagus (beech), Betula (birch), Quercus (oak), Populus (poplar)
- Primarily benefit woody and tree species

**Notable Genera:**
- *Amanita* (some species)
- *Boletus*
- *Russula*
- *Lactarius*
- *Tuber* (truffles)

**Evolutionary History:**
- Developed during the Jurassic period
- First fossil evidence: 48.7 million years ago (Eocene)

#### 3.1.2 Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungi (AMF)
**Characteristics:**
- Form tree-like structures (arbuscules) inside root cells
- Create vesicles for nutrient storage and exchange
- Most common type of mycorrhiza
- Found on over 70% of all vascular plants (80% of plant families)

**Phylum:** Glomeromycota

**Notable Genera:**
- *Glomus*
- *Rhizophagus*
- *Funneliformis*
- *Gigaspora*

**Evolutionary History:**
- Earliest mycorrhizal type
- Evidence dates back 460 million years
- Likely facilitated plant colonization of land

#### 3.1.3 Ericoid Mycorrhizae
**Characteristics:**
- Specialized for Ericaceae family (heaths, blueberries, rhododendrons)
- Penetrate root hair cells
- Particularly efficient at nitrogen acquisition in acidic soils

**Associated Plants:**
- *Vaccinium* (blueberries, cranberries)
- *Rhododendron*
- *Calluna* (heather)
- *Erica*

#### 3.1.4 Orchid Mycorrhizae
**Characteristics:**
- Obligate symbiosis for orchid germination
- Fungi provide carbon to non-photosynthetic orchid seeds
- Relationship continues throughout orchid life

**Notable Fungal Genera:**
- *Rhizoctonia*
- *Tulasnella*
- *Ceratobasidium*

### 3.2 Saprotrophic Fungi
**Ecological Role:** Primary decomposers; break down dead organic matter

**Notable Groups:**

#### White Rot Fungi
- Decompose both lignin and cellulose
- All bioluminescent fungi are white rot species
- Examples: *Pleurotus* (oyster mushrooms), *Trametes versicolor* (turkey tail)

#### Brown Rot Fungi
- Preferentially decompose cellulose, leave lignin
- Create "charcoal-like" remains
- Examples: *Serpula lacrymans*, *Postia placenta*

#### Soft Rot Fungi
- Decompose cellulose in wet conditions
- Often Ascomycetes
- Important in aquatic ecosystems

### 3.3 Parasitic Fungi

#### 3.3.1 Plant Pathogens
- *Fusarium* species (wilts, rots)
- *Phytophthora* (water molds, not true fungi but similar)
- *Armillaria* (honey fungus—parasitic and saprotrophic)
- *Ophiostoma* (Dutch elm disease)

#### 3.3.2 Animal Pathogens
- *Ophiocordyceps* (zombie ant fungi—~320 species, ~35 manipulate behavior)
- *Beauveria bassiana* (insect pathogen)
- *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis* (chytrid—amphibian decline)
- *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* (white-nose syndrome in bats)

#### 3.3.3 Fungal Parasites of Fungi
- *Trichoderma* species (mycoparasites)
- *Hypomyces* (lobster mushroom creator)

### 3.4 Slime Molds (Myxomycetes)

**Note:** Slime molds are not true fungi but share many characteristics and are traditionally studied by mycologists.

#### 3.4.1 Plasmodial Slime Molds (Myxomycetes)
**Characteristics:**
- Multinucleate single cells (plasmodia)
- Can grow to several square meters
- Exhibit primitive intelligence

**Notable Species:**
- *Physarum polycephalum* (model organism for intelligence studies)
- *Fuligo septica* (dog vomit slime mold)
- *Stemonitis* species

#### 3.4.2 Cellular Slime Molds (Dictyostelids)
**Characteristics:**
- Individual amoebae aggregate when food is scarce
- Form multicellular "slugs" that migrate
- Differentiate into spore-bearing structures

**Notable Species:**
- *Dictyostelium discoideum* (model organism)

### 3.5 Entheogenic Species

#### 3.5.1 Psilocybin-Producing Mushrooms
**Active Compounds:** Psilocybin, psilocin, baeocystin

**Notable Genera and Species:**
- *Psilocybe cubensis* (most commonly cultivated)
- *Psilocybe semilanceata* (liberty cap)
- *Psilocybe mexicana* (used in María Sabina ceremonies)
- *Psilocybe caerulescens* (Mazatec sacred mushroom)
- *Panaeolus* species

**Neurological Effects:**
- 5-HT2A receptor agonism
- Reduces default mode network (DMN) connectivity
- Increases neuroplasticity through BDNF pathway stimulation
- Promotes ego dissolution and enhanced connectivity between brain regions

**Therapeutic Research:**
- Treatment-resistant depression
- End-of-life anxiety
- Addiction (alcohol, tobacco)
- PTSD

#### 3.5.2 Amanita muscaria (Fly Agaric)
**Active Compounds:** Muscimol, ibotenic acid

**Characteristics:**
- Iconic red cap with white spots
- GABAergic action (different from psilocybin)
- Requires drying to convert ibotenic acid to muscimol

**Traditional Use:**
- Siberian shamanic practices (6000-4000 BCE or earlier)
- Urine recycling to extend effects
- Associated with Koryak people of Kamchatka Peninsula

#### 3.5.3 Ergot Fungi
**Species:** *Claviceps purpurea*

**Significance:**
- Source of lysergic acid (precursor to LSD)
- Possible component of Eleusinian Mysteries (kykeon)
- Historical role in St. Anthony's Fire

### 3.6 Medicinal Fungi

#### 3.6.1 Lion's Mane (*Hericium erinaceus*)
**Bioactive Compounds:**
- Hericenones (from fruiting body)
- Erinacines (from mycelia)
- Polysaccharides (beta-glucans)

**Neurological Effects:**
- Stimulates nerve growth factor (NGF) synthesis

**Clinical Research:**
- Improved cognitive function in mild cognitive impairment (16-week trials)

**Immunomodulatory Effects:**

#### 3.6.3 Turkey Tail (*Trametes versicolor*)

**Uses:**

**Compounds:**

#### 3.6.5 Chaga (*Inonotus obliquus*)

### 3.7 Bioluminescent Fungi

**Statistics:**
- Over 125 known bioluminescent species
- Five evolutionary lineages
- All Agaricales (mushroom-forming)
- All white rot fungi

#### Evolutionary Lineages

**Omphalotus Lineage (18 species)**
- *Omphalotus olearius* (jack-o'-lantern)
- *Neonothopanus nambi*

**Armillaria Lineage (14 species)**
- *Armillaria mellea* (most widely distributed)
- *Armillaria gallica* (only mycelia luminescent)
- Distribution: Asia, Europe, North America, South Africa

**Mycenoid Lineage (96 species)**
- *Mycena chlorophos* (tropical Asia, Australasia)
- *Panellus stipticus* (North American strains only)
- *Roridomyces* species

**Lucentipes Lineage (3 species)**
- Recently discovered
- Tropical distribution

**Eoscyphella Lineage (1 species)**
- Most recently discovered

**Bioluminescence Mechanism:**
- Common enzymatic pathway across all lineages
- Luciferin precursor: hispidin
- Luciferin: 3-hydroxyhispidin
- Emission wavelength: 520-530 nm
- Follows circadian rhythm

### 3.8 Lichenized Fungi

- Mostly Ascomycetes

**Ecological Roles:**
- Pioneer species on bare rock
- Nitrogen fixation (cyanobacteria partners)
- Air quality indicators

## 4. Indigenous Knowledge Integration

### 4.1 Mazatec Mushroom Traditions (María Sabina)

#### Historical Context
María Sabina Magdalena García (1894-1985) was a Mazatec sabia (wise woman) who lived in Huautla de Jiménez, Oaxaca, Mexico. She was the most renowned practitioner of the traditional velada ceremony.

#### The Velada Ceremony
**Definition:** A nocturnal healing ceremony centered on psilocybin mushroom consumption

**Sacred Mushrooms Used:**
- *Psilocybe caerulescens* ("landslide mushroom")
- *Psilocybe cubensis*
- Known locally as "ndi xijtho" ("the little ones that sprout") or "holy children"

**Ceremony Elements:**
- Conducted at night in darkness
- Prayer, chanting, incense, symbolic offerings
- Not recreational but therapeutic and spiritual
- Four days of sexual abstinence and special diet before and after
- Mushrooms viewed as conscious entities capable of communicating wisdom

**Purposes:**
- Healing illness
- Divination
- Communication with spirits
- Maintaining collective myths

#### Contact with Western World
**R. Gordon Wasson's Visit (1955):**
- J.P. Morgan vice president traveled to Huautla de Jiménez
- María Sabina led him through a velada
- Published "Seeking the Magic Mushroom" in *Life* magazine (1957)

**Consequences:**
- Mass influx of Western seekers (Dylan, Lennon, scientists, hippies)
- Community blamed María Sabina for misfortune
- Traditional practices disrupted and "defiled"
- No benefit to Mazatec people despite multi-billion dollar psilocybin industry
- Indigenous intellectual property concerns remain unresolved

**María Sabina's Reflection:** "From the moment the foreigners arrived to search for God, the saint children lost their purity. They lost their force; the foreigners spoiled them."

### 4.2 Siberian Amanita Practices

#### Historical Depth
- Use may date to 6,000-10,000 years ago
- Predates crossing of Bering Strait into Americas
- Documented among multiple Siberian indigenous groups

#### The Koryak People (Kamchatka Peninsula)
**Cultural Beliefs:**
- *Amanita muscaria* is a "divine offering" from Big Raven
- Big Raven: first shaman and progenitor of humankind
- Legend: Big Raven discovered mushroom's properties while trying to move a whale

**Shamanic Purposes:**
- Medicine and healing
- Communication with gods and spirits
- Divination
- Maintaining collective myths
- Vision quests
- Out-of-body journeys to spirit world

#### Preparation and Consumption
**Drying Process:**
- Critical for safety and efficacy
- Converts ibotenic acid to muscimol
- Often dried over fire, sometimes in socks
- Reduces unpleasant physical effects

**Urine Recycling:**
- Muscimol passes through body largely unmetabolized
- Drinking urine of consumer extends effects
- Reduces unpleasant physical symptoms
- Both human and reindeer urine used

#### Reindeer Connection
- Reindeer actively seek out and consume *Amanita muscaria*
- Exhibit "flying" or dancing behavior after consumption
- Herders sometimes collected intoxicated reindeer urine
- Possible origin of "flying reindeer" mythology

#### Current Status
- Traditional practices diminished due to Russian colonization and Orthodox Christianity
- Stigmatization of ancestral customs
- Pockets of cultural resistance remain
- Represents cultural identity preservation efforts

### 4.3 Aboriginal Australian Fungal Knowledge

#### Traditional Practices
For thousands of years, Aboriginal Australians developed extensive fungal knowledge passed orally through generations. Written records began in the 19th century through European observers.

**Uses:**
- Food (primarily truffles)
- Medicine
- Body decoration
- Fire-carrying

**Historical Documentation:**
- George Grey (1841) reported seven fungi species eaten by Aborigines in Western Australia

#### Desert Truffles
**Ecological Knowledge Required:**
- Sporadic occurrence depending on rainfall
- Specific soil conditions and mycorrhizal hosts needed
- Women traditionally primary truffle hunters

**Hunting Techniques:**
- Look for cracks or humps in soil from truffle expansion
- Extraction with digging sticks

**Preparation:**
- Eaten raw
- Baked or roasted in ashes
- Strong mushroom flavor

**Medicinal Uses of Desert Truffles:**
- Fluid applied to sores and sore eyes
- Rubbed into armpits
- Rubbed into hair to prevent growth

#### Blackfellow's Bread (*Laccocephalum mylittae*)
- Large, dark underground fruiting body
- Important traditional food source
- One of few species with detailed ethnomycological records

#### Phellinus Species (Bracket Fungi)
**Medicinal Applications:**
- Smoke from burning fruiting bodies inhaled for sore throats
- Charred scrapings drunk with water for:
  - Coughing
  - Sore throats
  - "Bad chests"
  - Fevers
  - Diarrhea

#### Pisolithus Species
- Used on wounds when tarry consistency
- Eaten when very young (likely emergency food)

#### Fire-Carrying
- In Tasmania, South Australia, and Western Australia, specific fungi used as tinder
- Would smolder all day, allowing fire transport between camps

#### Current State of Knowledge
- Much knowledge likely lost due to European colonization
- Large geographic blank areas with no recorded uses
- Potential for ethnomycological fieldwork
- Some knowledge may still exist in communities

### 4.5 Fungal Symbolism in Cultures

#### Fairy Rings
- European folklore: Dancing grounds for fairies/elves

### 5.1 Mycelium as Biological Computer

#### Theoretical Framework
Fungal mycelium functions as a distributed computing network with no central processor, demonstrating emergent computational properties through collective behavior of millions of hyphal tips.

**Computational Properties:**
- Massively parallel processing
- Fault tolerance through redundancy
- Adaptive reconfiguration
- Energy-efficient computation

**Information Processing:**
- Sensing through chemoreception at hyphal tips
- Signal transduction via cytoplasmic streaming
- Memory storage in network architecture
- Decision-making through collective dynamics

### 5.2 Unconventional Computing with Fungi

#### Andrew Adamatzky's Research
Andrew Adamatzky, Director of the Unconventional Computing Laboratory at University of the West of England, has pioneered fungal computing research.

**Physarum Machine Concept:**
- Programmable amorphous biological computer
- Uses vegetative state of *P. polycephalum*
- Programmed through spatial configurations of attracting/repelling gradients
- "Green" and environmentally friendly

**Demonstrated Applications:**
1. **Logical Gates:** XOR, NOR gates using tube flow
2. **Electronic Devices:**
   - Memristors
   - Diodes
   - Transistors
   - Wires
3. **Sensors:**
   - Chemical sensors
   - Tactile sensors
4. **Memory Devices:** Based on tube network persistence

**Current Research:**
- Over 40 prototypes developed
- Shift to fungal mycelium computing
- "Computing houses" grown from mycelium
- Functionalized fungal matter responding to light, temperature, pollution

### 5.4 Network Resilience Patterns

Fungal networks demonstrate remarkable resilience through:
